The Origin

The Question That Built the System

There was a client — a serious one, a former athlete who had put time in the gym for years — who came to train with an injury that had been unresolved for months. Not catastrophic. Just stubborn. The kind that keeps coming back because no one has addressed the thing underneath it.

The standard approach in that situation is to work around it: modify the exercise, reduce the load, be patient. That approach produces the same result it always produces — someone who can train until they can't, and then can't again.

"The injury wasn't the problem. The injury was the symptom. The problem was that the body had never been taught the prerequisites for the demands being placed on it."

That realization forced a rebuild of every assumption about how training should be sequenced. Not because the standard approach is wrong about everything — but because it starts in the middle. It assumes a foundation that most people don't actually have.

Breath control is assumed. Foundational movement quality is assumed. Stability through range is assumed. The myofascial slings that transmit force across the body — never discussed. The result is a training culture that loads dysfunction, programs intensity on a foundation of compromise, and then wonders why people plateau, get hurt, and quit.

The Monger Method was built to fix the sequence. Not to replace intensity — intensity has its place, and it's earned — but to ensure that when intensity arrives, the body is ready to receive it.

Seven sections. One order. Build a body you can trust.

The Values
The Values
What This Is Built On

How I Think About Training

01

Honesty Over Hype

There is no hack. There is no shortcut. There is a logical sequence, and either you follow it or you don't. The Monger Method is built on the former — and it does not pretend otherwise.

02

Education, Not Instruction

The goal is not compliance. The goal is understanding. A client who understands why the sequence exists will follow it more reliably than one who is simply told to.

03

The Body Is Integrated

Isolated muscle training has its place. But a body that moves — in sport, in life, under load — moves as a unit. Training must reflect that reality.

04

Quality Before Quantity

AMQR. As Many Quality Reps. One session that teaches your nervous system something is worth more than ten that only exhaust it. Volume is a tool, not a goal.

05

Compassion With Standards

The clients who benefit most from this system are the ones who have been failed by other approaches. That demands patience. It does not demand lower standards.

06

Strength Is a Reward

You don't start with load. You earn load by building a system that is capable of using it safely and effectively. That framing changes everything about how training feels.
